The Kremlin’s war economy: sanctions and resilience

By Maria Kuznetsova June 15, 2025 12 min read 24k views

YEKATERINBURG — In the basement of a converted Soviet-era factory, a machine the size of a bus etches structures smaller than a virus onto silicon wafers. It runs 24 hours a day, seven days a week, and its output is destined for Russian missiles and drones.

This is the new face of Russia's war economy. Two years after Western sanctions were imposed following the invasion of Ukraine, factories across the country are running at full capacity. But at what cost? And can this momentum be sustained?

The industrial pivot

According to newly obtained data, Russian manufacturing output has actually increased by 8% in the past 12 months, with defense-related industries growing by nearly 30%. The Uralvagonzavod plant, famous for producing T-90 tanks, is now operating three shifts.

We're seeing a level of industrial mobilization not witnessed since 1941. The entire economy is being reconfigured for war.

But the boom comes with severe side effects. Inflation is running at an estimated 15%, and the central bank has raised interest rates to 18%. The ruble, while stable, is propped up by capital controls and mandatory conversion of export revenues.

Human cost

Labor shortages are acute. Over 500,000 men have either been mobilized or fled the country. Women and pensioners are filling factory roles, but wages are rising faster than productivity. "We're hiring anyone with a pulse," one factory manager admitted.

The long-term picture remains uncertain. While current production meets battlefield needs, technological degradation continues. Microchips are being stripped from dishwashers and refrigerators, and advanced machinery cannot be replaced due to export controls.
